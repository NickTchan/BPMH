# Patient Counselling Techniques for Medication Changes

## Purpose

To ensure patients genuinely understand their discharge medication plan — including what to take, when, why, and what has changed — using evidence-based communication techniques that compensate for the well-documented limitations of verbal-only information delivery.

## When to Use

This procedure applies whenever a patient's medication regimen has changed during their hospital stay and they are being discharged. It is a sub-procedure of **[PRC-004](../procedures/supply-medicines-information.md)** and should be performed as part of Step 3 (Communicate to the Patient).

## Procedure Steps

### Step 1: Prepare the Written Materials
- Print the discharge medication list before beginning the counselling session
- Ensure the list clearly identifies:
  - **Continued medications** — unchanged from pre-admission or current inpatient regimen
  - **Commenced medications** — new additions with indication
  - **Ceased medications** — what was stopped and why
  - **Changed medications** — what changed (dose, frequency, formulation) and the reason
- Have the patient's actual medication containers available if possible (for visual comparison)

### Step 2: Deliver the Counselling Session
- Sit at the patient's level; minimise distractions
- Work through the medication list systematically, addressing each change
- Use **contrastive, patient-friendly language**:
  - *"This is the same as that drug you were taking, it just looks different"*
  - *"We don't want you to take that one anymore — here's what to take instead"*
  - *"This one is new — it's for your blood pressure"*
- Avoid medical jargon; use plain language for indications (e.g., "water tablet" alongside "diuretic" if the patient uses that term)

### Step 3: Apply the Teach-Back Method
- After explaining the medication plan, ask the patient to repeat the instructions **in their own words**:
  - *"Can you tell me in your own words what medicines you'll be taking when you go home?"*
  - *"What will you do if you're not sure about a medicine?"*
- Do not ask yes/no questions like "Do you understand?" — these do not verify comprehension
- If the patient cannot accurately describe their plan, re-explain and re-test
- Document that teach-back was performed and the patient's level of understanding

### Step 4: Provide Hard-Copy Documentation
- Hand the patient the printed discharge medication list
- Emphasise that this document is their reference — verbal instructions alone are insufficient because the vast majority of people do not retain spoken information accurately
- Point out key sections: what to continue, what's new, what's stopped
- If the patient uses a dose administration aid (Webster pack), explain that their community pharmacy will repack it based on the updated plan

### Step 5: Address Special Circumstances

#### Cognitive Impairment
- Involve a carer or family member in the counselling session
- Provide the carer with a copy of the medication list
- Simplify explanations; use visual aids where possible
- Consider whether the patient has capacity to manage their own medications independently

#### Non-English Speakers
- Use a professional interpreter (in-person or telephone/video) — do not rely on family members for clinical translation
- Ensure the written medication list is in the patient's preferred language where possible
- Allow extra time for the session

#### Telehealth/VMM Discharge Counselling
- Require camera-on where possible to assess patient engagement and understanding
- Compensate for the inability to physically show medications by using clear verbal descriptions
- Follow up with hard-copy documentation sent by post or made available for collection
- In audio-only settings, use more targeted probing questions to verify comprehension

### Step 6: Follow-Up Check
- Ask the patient if they have any questions after the counselling session
- Do not assume silence means understanding — explicitly invite questions
- Provide contact information for the pharmacy or clinical team if questions arise after discharge

## Output

A patient who has demonstrated understanding of their discharge medication plan through teach-back, supported by written documentation and (where applicable) carer or interpreter involvement.
