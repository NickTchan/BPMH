# Paediatric Formulation Handling

## Purpose

To guide clinicians in identifying and documenting formulation-specific issues that affect medication administration in paediatric patients.

## Why Formulation Matters in Paediatrics

Children often cannot swallow tablets, and caregivers may have strong preferences for liquid formulations. The same medication may be available in multiple forms (tablet, capsule, liquid, dispersible, suppository), and the choice of formulation directly affects:

- **Dose accuracy:** Liquid formulations require volume-based dosing; tablets require weight-based calculation
- **Adherence:** A child who refuses a crushed tablet may not receive the medication at all
- **Bioavailability:** Some formulations should not be crushed or split (e.g., modified-release preparations)
- **Palatability:** Taste affects willingness to take the medication, especially in young children

## Formulation Assessment Checklist

During the BPMH interview, for each medication:

1. **What formulation is the patient actually taking?** (tablet, liquid, dispersible, suppository, etc.)
2. **Can the patient swallow this formulation?** If not, how is it administered? (crushed, mixed with food, etc.)
3. **Is the formulation appropriate for the patient's age and ability?**
4. **Are there any formulation-specific administration instructions?** (e.g., "do not crush", "take with food")
5. **Does the caregiver have a preference?** (e.g., prefers liquid over crushed tablets)

## Common Paediatric Formulation Issues

| Issue | Clinical Implication | Action |
|-------|---------------------|--------|
| Tablet crushed without checking | Modified-release or enteric-coated tablets may lose their protective mechanism | Verify formulation type before recommending crushing |
| Liquid dose measured with household spoon | Inaccurate dosing — household spoons vary widely in volume | Recommend oral syringe or calibrated measuring device |
| Multiple liquid medications with different concentrations | Confusion between mg/mL concentrations leads to dosing errors | Document concentration explicitly (e.g., "paracetamol 120mg/5mL") |
| Child refuses taste of liquid formulation | Non-adherence due to palatability | Explore alternative formulations or flavouring options |

> **Tacit cue:** When a parent says *"they can take it crushed"* or *"we prefer the liquid,"* this is a formulation preference signal. Document it — it may explain adherence patterns or inform discharge planning.
