# Clinical Change Rationale Cues

## Purpose

To help clinicians recognise and document the reasons why medications were started, stopped, or changed - a critical component of accurate BPMH that distinguishes intentional changes from unintentional discrepancies.

## Why This Matters

When a medication on the patient's history does not appear in current orders (or vice versa), the clinician must determine whether this represents:
- An **intentional clinical decision** (e.g., prescriber ceased a medication due to improved condition)
- An **unintentional discrepancy** (e.g., medication omitted in error during transfer of care)

Understanding the clinical rationale is essential for correct classification and patient safety.

## Cues to Listen For

During the BPMH interview, patients and caregivers often volunteer information about medication changes. Recognise these cues:

| Cue | What It Signals | Follow-Up Question |
|-----|----------------|-------------------|
| *"The doctor changed it last time"* | Intentional change by prescriber | *"Do you remember what they changed it to?"* |
| *"We stopped giving that because..."* | Intentional cessation (may or may not be prescriber-directed) | *"Who made that decision - the doctor or you?"* |
| *"They gave us a different one at the pharmacy"* | Substitution (brand/generic or therapeutic) | *"Do you still have the old box or the new one?"* |
| *"It wasn't working"* | Therapeutic failure or perceived ineffectiveness | *"What made you think it wasn't working?"* |
| *"It made them sick/unwell"* | Adverse reaction or side effect | *"What happened? When did it start?"* |
| *"We ran out and couldn't get a repeat"* | Access barrier (not a clinical decision) | *"When did you run out? Have you tried to get a repeat?"* |
| *"The specialist said to stop it"* | Specialist-directed change | *"Do you have a letter from the specialist?"* |

## Documentation Guidance

When a medication change is identified:

1. **Document the change:** What was the previous medication? What is the current one (if any)?
2. **Document the rationale:** Why was it changed? (clinical decision, side effect, access issue, etc.)
3. **Document the source:** Who made the decision? (GP, specialist, patient, pharmacist)
4. **Flag for prescriber review:** If the rationale is unclear or the change appears unintentional, flag it for the treating team

> **Tacit cue:** Patients and caregivers often describe medication changes in narrative form rather than clinical terms. *"They switched him to the pink one"* may mean a formulation change, a brand substitution, or a completely different medication. Always verify by asking for the container or checking the prescription.
