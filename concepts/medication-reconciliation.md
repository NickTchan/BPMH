# Medication Reconciliation

## Definition

**Medication reconciliation** is a formal, structured process for confirming a patient's current medicines at every transfer of care. It is defined as "the procedure of ensuring that patients receive all intended medications and that accurate, current, and comprehensive medication information follows them at all transfers of care" [REF: NSW Health PD2022_032].

## Four-Step Process (NSW CEC Framework)

1. **Collect** information to compile a list of the patient's current medicines (with clear name, dose, and frequency for each)
2. **Confirm** the accuracy of that list to achieve a Best Possible Medication History (BPMH)
3. **Compare** the BPMH against prescribed medicines at every transfer of care, identifying and resolving any discrepancies
4. **Supply** accurate medicines information to the patient and the next care provider

## Policy Context

- **NSQHS Standards:** The Medication Safety Standard (Action 4.10) requires health service organisations to implement medication reconciliation at admission, transfer, and discharge.
- **NSW Health Policy:** PD2022_032 mandates that all NSW public health facilities implement formal medication reconciliation procedures.
- **WHO High5s:** Medication reconciliation is one of the WHO's High5s patient safety challenges, with a Standard Operating Protocol for implementation.

## Application

Medication reconciliation is the overarching process within which the BPMH (Step 1–2) operates. The BPMH is the *input* to reconciliation; reconciliation is the *comparison and resolution* process.
