# BPMH as Diagnostic Conversation

## Description

The expert's internal model of the BPMH interview is not a checklist exercise but a **diagnostic conversation** — a dynamic, adaptive process where each answer informs the next question.

## Model Structure

The expert mentally constructs a picture of the patient's medication ecosystem:
1. **What should this patient be on?** Based on their diagnoses (e.g., a patient with heart failure, diabetes, and atrial fibrillation *should* be on specific medication classes).
2. **What does the patient say they're on?** The patient's self-report.
3. **What do the records say?** Pharmacy, GP, and hospital records.
4. **Where are the gaps?** Discrepancies between (1), (2), and (3) become the focus of targeted probing.

## How the Expert Uses This Model

- When a patient with diabetes does not mention any diabetes medications, the expert probes specifically: *"Do you take anything for your blood sugar?"*
- When a patient mentions a medication the expert has not heard of, the expert asks for the container or describes the tablet to identify it.
- When the patient's narrative suggests they are taking medications "as needed" for chronic conditions, the expert probes to understand the actual pattern of use.

## BPMH as Integrated Clinical Assessment

A BPMH is not solely a medication inventory — it is a diagnostic conversation that:

- Reveals adherence patterns through indirect questioning rather than direct interrogation
- Uncovers clinical context (recent illnesses, family exposures, symptom patterns) that explains why medications were started, stopped, or changed
- Identifies formulation and administration barriers that affect actual medication use
- Builds rapport that surfaces information patients and caregivers would not volunteer in direct questioning

The expert explores symptoms (e.g., abdominal pain, reduced intake, decreased urination) *alongside* medication enquiry, demonstrating that understanding the clinical picture is inseparable from understanding the medication history.

## Conversational Progression Pattern

Effective BPMH interviews follow a natural progression that deepens both information quality and rapport:

1. **Broad parent-directed enquiry:** Open questions such as *"Tell me about..."* or *"How has [the patient] been feeling?"*
2. **Specific follow-up probing:** Targeted questions based on parent responses — dose timing, formulation preferences, recent changes
3. **Child-directed validation:** When age-appropriate, direct engagement with the patient to corroborate or supplement the caregiver's account (e.g., *"Do you want to tell us a bit about how you've been feeling?"*)

This progression moves from wide to narrow, and from caregiver to patient, building a complete clinical picture.

## Boundary Conditions

This model breaks down when:
- The patient is unconscious or unable to communicate
- The patient has no regular medications
- The patient's diagnoses are unknown at the time of interview

## Gap Note

Sources describe the interview questions but do not articulate the expert's **internal model** of how they construct and use this mental picture. This is tacit knowledge.
