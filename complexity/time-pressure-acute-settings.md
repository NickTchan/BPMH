# Time Pressure in Acute Settings

## Description

In Emergency Departments and acute medical admissions, the pressure to rapidly assess and treat patients competes with the time required for a thorough BPMH interview and verification process.

## Why It Makes the Domain Difficult

- A thorough BPMH interview can take 15–30 minutes for a complex patient
- ED clinicians may have only minutes to obtain initial medication information
- The tension between speed and accuracy creates a risk of incomplete BPMH
- Verification sources (pharmacy, GP) may not be immediately accessible

## Expert Adaptation

Experts adapt by:
- Obtaining a "rapid BPMH" — focusing on high-risk medications first (anticoagulants, insulin, anti-epileptics, cardiac medications)
- Using immediately accessible sources (eMR history, My Health Record) for initial verification
- Flagging the BPMH as "preliminary" and scheduling follow-up verification when time permits
- Delegating detailed verification to pharmacy when available

## Novice Vulnerability

Novices may either rush through the interview (producing an incomplete BPMH) or spend too much time on low-risk medications while missing critical ones.

## Instructional Implications

CSP agents should teach a tiered approach: rapid assessment of high-risk medications first, followed by comprehensive verification when time allows.
