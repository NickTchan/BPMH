# Expert Cues for Recognising Clinically Obvious Medication Changes

## Gap Description

Experts can rapidly triage medication discrepancies into "obviously intentional" vs. "needs investigation" based on the patient's clinical context. The **specific clinical cues** that trigger this recognition are not documented.

## Why This Matters

This pattern recognition is what separates experts from novices. Novices treat all discrepancies equally, requiring prescriber confirmation for each one. Experts save time and focus attention where it matters by instantly recognising clinically obvious changes.

## What We Know

Expert practitioners have identified the following concrete red-flag cues that suggest a discrepancy may be **unintentional** and warrants escalation to the prescriber for confirmation:

1. **"Can't find any information about the change"** — No documentation of the medication change in the medication chart or progress notes. If a change was deliberate, there should be some record of it.
2. **"Indication doesn't make sense for them"** — The clinical indication for a medication does not match the patient's presentation, diagnosis, or profile. This suggests the medication may have been prescribed in error.
3. **"Starting dose was very abnormal for a new medication"** — A dose that falls outside standard pharmacological ranges for a newly initiated medication is a pharmacological outlier and may indicate a prescribing error.
4. **"Maybe the drug had gone to the wrong person"** — In rare cases, a medication may have been dispensed to or charted for the wrong patient entirely, representing a patient identification error.

Each of these cues triggers escalation to the prescriber for confirmation before the discrepancy is classified.

## What We Don't Know

- How experts handle borderline cases where the clinical rationale is plausible but not certain
- Whether this pattern recognition is medication-specific or diagnosis-specific
- The full set of clinical cue → medication change patterns beyond the red flags above

## CTA Priority: 4

This is a core perceptual discrimination that novices universally miss and that directly affects efficiency and safety.
