# Recognise Clinical Rationale for Medication Change

## Description

The expert's ability to look at a medication discrepancy and immediately recognise whether the patient's clinical context provides an obvious rationale for the change — without needing to ask the prescriber.

## Perceptual Cue

The expert reads the patient's admission diagnosis, current clinical status, and recent investigation results, then mentally maps these against the medication discrepancy.

## Threshold

- **Obvious intentional change:** The patient is admitted with acute kidney injury → metformin has been ceased → expert recognises this as clinically appropriate and likely intentional.
- **Unclear:** The patient is admitted with a fractured neck of femur → their statin has been ceased → expert cannot immediately see the rationale → treats as potentially unintentional.

## What Novices Miss

Novices tend to treat all discrepancies equally, requiring prescriber confirmation for each one. Experts use their clinical knowledge to rapidly triage discrepancies into "obviously intentional" vs. "needs investigation," saving time and focusing attention where it matters.

## How This Knowledge Was Developed

Inferred from the WHO High5s SOP's emphasis on clinical judgement in discrepancy classification, and from the general principle that experienced clinicians develop pattern recognition for common clinical scenarios.
